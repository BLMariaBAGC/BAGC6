Discovering Your Ikigai

A guided journey to your reason for being

Introduction

The Secret to a Long and Happy Life

The secret to a long and happy life is to live with purpose every day. Living a life filled with meaning and value can make you happier, more content, and more resilient through hard times. It can also deepen your capacity to positively influence the lives of those around you.

People find meaning from many different sources: relationships, work, spiritual practice, creative pursuits, or hobbies. There is no single path.

What gives you a sense of meaning or purpose in your life?
What Is Ikigai?

Pronounced: ee-key-guy. Ikigai is a Japanese practice that has guided generations towards lives of deep fulfilment and longevity. Roughly translated, it means "a reason for being" or "a reason for getting up in the morning."

Why it matters: Finding your ikigai provides a reason for living, even when faced with adversity and hardship. Communities in Japan where ikigai is deeply practised are among the longest-living in the world.

The Four Ingredients

Your ikigai lives at the point where all four of these circles converge.

What You Love

The passions, interests, and activities that light you up from within.

What the World Needs

The contributions, services, or support that your community and society truly value.

What You Are Good At

Your natural strengths, developed abilities, and areas of genuine competence.

What You Can Be Paid For

The skills and talents that others are willing to reward financially.

Step 1 of 4
What Do You Love?

Passion is essential to finding your ikigai. Without it, you may struggle to sustain the energy and motivation needed to pursue your reason for living, especially during difficult times.

What do you genuinely feel passionate about?

Take a few minutes to reflect on the things you love across all areas of life: your work, your family, volunteer activities, personal interests, and hobbies. Perhaps you feel a deep connection to science, the arts, nature, teaching, or travel. Allow yourself to think broadly and without judgement.

There are no right or wrong answers. Write whatever is true for you, however large or small it may seem.

Step 2 of 4
What Does the World Need?

Humans are deeply social animals. When we know that we are needed by others, we feel useful, valuable, and full of purpose. This step helps you discover what you have to give to the world around you.

What do your friends, family, and immediate community need?

Thinking about global needs can feel overwhelming, so begin locally. These needs can include almost anything: a product, a service, emotional support, creative work, or simply showing up for one single person who needs you. Small contributions matter enormously.

Start with the people closest to you. What do they come to you for? What problems do you see around you that you wish you could help with?

Step 3 of 4
What Are You Good At?

Discovering what you are good at involves meaningful self-evaluation, and it is worth approaching this step with both humility and self-compassion.

What am I good at?

Before answering, take time to consider your skills, your strengths, and everything you are capable of doing well, no matter how large or small. Perhaps you have a talent that comes naturally, that others find helpful, or that you have worked hard over many years to develop and refine.

Excessively high standards can be counterproductive. You may be more capable than you give yourself credit for. Allow yourself to claim your strengths honestly.

Step 4 of 4
What Can You Be Paid For?

This question is about sustainability. For your ikigai to be truly liveable, there needs to be a way for it to support your life financially, whether now or in the future.

What skills or services are others willing to pay for?

Consider both what you are currently paid for and what you could be paid for if you developed it further. Think about what people have offered you money for, asked you to do professionally, or turned to you for because of your expertise.

If earning from something feels distant right now, that is fine. Write what you could one day be paid for, not only what you are paid for today.

Step 5: Overlaps
Finding Your Overlapping Responses

Now look carefully across everything you have written. You may notice that some answers appear in more than one circle, or that certain answers are closely related to one another. These overlapping areas are where the real magic begins.

Any response that fits into all four circles belongs at the very centre of your chart. That is your ikigai.

Where your love and your strengths meet. You may feel deep satisfaction here.

Where your passion meets genuine need. You are likely to feel a deep sense of purpose here.

Where your skills meet financial reward. Competence and comfort, though meaning may still be missing.

Where need meets financial reward. Sustainable and impactful, though love for the work may be missing.

If something fits all four, write it here. This is your reason for being.

Step 6: The Gap
Finding the Missing Circle

To find your ikigai, you must first identify which circles may be missing from your picture, and then consider how to address them. Look carefully at your responses.

Is there an activity or theme that appears in more than one circle but not all four? The missing circle is not a gap to feel discouraged by. It is an invitation to grow.

Step 7: Growing Towards Ikigai
Addressing the Missing Circle

For every activity or theme in your table, consider the circles that are currently missing. Work through the sections below that are relevant to your own responses. Be patient and honest with yourself throughout this reflection.

If You Do Not Love This Activity

Are there specific aspects that drain you? Could those aspects be delegated, shared, or made more manageable?

List three things you find positive about this activity, however small.

Are there areas within this activity that intrigue you? Following curiosity is often the first step towards genuine passion.

In what ways has this activity helped you develop as a person?

If You Do Not Feel Skilled Enough

Is it possible you are more capable than you give yourself credit for?

Which skills do you already possess? Which need development?

Who excels at this activity? What strengths do they have that you could cultivate?

Is there a mentor who could guide and accelerate your growth?

If You Are Not Getting Paid for This

What are the main reasons you are not currently paid for this? Naming the barriers is the first step to dismantling them.

How have other people monetised this activity or something closely related?

Could you earn from this in a small way right now, alongside your current work?

List three ways you could move towards financial reward from this activity.

If This Is Not What the World Needs

What do the people in your immediate circle actually need? In what ways could this activity meet those needs?

Could you write, teach, host a workshop, or share your knowledge in a way that serves others?

Is this something you could teach locally or online? Sharing skills is one of the most direct ways to give back.

✓

Your Ikigai Journey Is Complete

You have invested time in understanding what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. That is a courageous and deeply intentional step.

Your Ikigai Is a Living Journey

If this exercise did not immediately reveal an activity that sits at the centre of all four circles, please do not be discouraged. Finding your ikigai is rarely a single moment of revelation. It is an ongoing, evolving process of self-reflection, curiosity, and courage.

Your ikigai is also likely to change as your circumstances, relationships, and values shift over time. This is not a weakness. It is simply the nature of a life lived with awareness.

Each time you return to this exercise, you will know yourself a little better than before.
