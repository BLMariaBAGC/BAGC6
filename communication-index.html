6 Steps to Improve Communication and Resolve Misunderstandings

A practical framework for difficult conversations

BAGC6 Executive Reset  ·  Beyond Limits
Misunderstandings are not a sign of a broken relationship. They are a sign that two people have different maps of the world and are each doing their best with the information their RAS made available. This framework gives you a structured, respectful way to address misunderstandings, restore clarity, and move forward without blame.
KEY RULE: Never use “but” or “however” in this conversation. Both words erase the message before them. Use “and” or start a new sentence instead.
1
Ask for Help

Begin by inviting the other person into the conversation as a collaborator, not an opponent. When you ask for help, you immediately lower their defences and signal that your intention is to understand, not to attack.

Suggested Phrases
I would love your help with something.
I wonder if you could help me understand something.
I would really value your perspective on something that is on my mind.
YOUR SITUATIONHow will you open this conversation? Write your opening sentence in your own words.
2
Specify the Misunderstanding

Be precise and factual. Name the specific event that triggered the misunderstanding, then describe the meaning your brain attached to it. This is not about accusing the other person. It is about showing them what happened inside your own head.

Suggested Phrases
When [specific event] happened, I misunderstood that to mean...
When you [describe the observable behaviour], my brain told me that...
I want to be honest. When [situation], I found myself thinking...
Example: “When you interrupted me during my presentation, I found myself thinking you were trying to undermine me. I know that may not be what was happening, and I would love to understand it better.”
YOUR SITUATIONWhat is the specific event? What meaning did your brain attach to it?
3
Acknowledge Their Good Intentions

This step is non-negotiable. Before you ask for anything, you must first demonstrate that you believe in the other person’s positive intentions. Nobody makes mistakes on purpose. When you say this out loud, you give them the emotional safety to be honest with you.

Suggested Phrases
I know this is not what you intended.
I know you would never deliberately want me to feel that way.
I trust your intentions completely, and I want to understand what was behind it.
I know who you are, and that is exactly why I want to clear this up.
YOUR SITUATIONHow will you acknowledge their good intentions in a way that feels genuine and specific to them?
4
Seek Clarity

Now invite them to share their perspective. Your job in this moment is not to respond or defend. It is to genuinely listen. Their answer will almost always reveal that their intention was entirely different from the meaning your RAS created. Let them finish without interruption.

Suggested Phrases
Could you help me understand what was happening for you at that moment?
I would really love to hear your perspective on what happened.
What was going on for you when you [describe the behaviour]?
YOUR SITUATIONWhat question will you ask them? Write it in your own words so it feels natural when you say it.
5
Appreciate Their Perspective

Once they have explained, validate what they have shared before you say anything else. Even if their explanation surprises you, your first response should always be appreciation. This confirms that they were right to be honest with you and reinforces the trust you have built.

Suggested Phrases
I really appreciate you explaining that.
Thank you. That makes complete sense now that I understand what was happening for you.
I appreciate your honesty. That gives me a very different picture.
That is really helpful. I can see now where the wires crossed.
YOUR SITUATIONWhat will you say to show genuine appreciation for what they share?
6
Make a Future Request

Close the conversation by making one specific, forward-facing request. This is not a demand or a complaint. It is an invitation to do something differently going forward. Frame it positively and make it actionable.

Suggested Phrases
Can I make a request for the future?
Going forward, it would mean a great deal to me if...
I would really appreciate it if, in future, you could...
Example: “I would appreciate it if you could wait until after I have finished presenting before offering corrections. It would help me feel supported in the room.”
YOUR SITUATIONWhat is your one specific, positive, forward-facing request? Write it out in full.

Keep the conversation positive. Maintain rapport at every stage.

Focus on understanding intentions, not assigning blame.
