Crafting Your Affirmations

Turning your strengths into daily declarations
Why this exercise is in Week 3

You now have a clearer picture of your human needs, your behavioural patterns, and where your life is in and out of balance. This exercise asks you to do something specific with that awareness: translate it into language your subconscious can work with.

Affirmations are not positive thinking. They are precision tools. When constructed from real, specific, personally identified strengths, they create new neurological pathways rather than paper over old ones. The difference between an affirmation that works and one that does not is the difference between surface language and language rooted in evidence.

What Affirmations Actually Are

An affirmation is a present-tense statement designed to replace a limiting belief with a more accurate and empowering one. The critical word is accurate. An affirmation you do not believe at some level does nothing. An affirmation grounded in a real strength you have already demonstrated will land differently, because part of you already knows it is true.

Research consistently shows that self-affirmation activates the brain's reward circuits, reduces the physiological stress response, and increases openness to behaviour change. This is not about feeling better in the moment. It is about rewiring the default narrative your nervous system runs.

The Five Principles

What separates an affirmation that works from one that does not

1
Ground it in a real strength

Go back to last week's work. What did the exercises reveal about you? Start there, not with aspiration. Evidence is the foundation.

2
Frame it toward what you want

State what you are moving toward, not what you are moving away from. The subconscious does not process negatives well. "I speak with clarity and conviction" works.

3
Use the present tense

State the affirmation as already true. "I am," not "I will be." This is not self-deception. It is directing the nervous system toward a state it is already capable of.

4
Be specific rather than grand

Vague affirmations produce vague results. "I am successful" gives the brain nothing. "I think clearly under pressure and make decisions from calm authority" gives it a precise target.

5
Add genuine emotional weight

The affirmation that lands is the one that produces a real felt response when you say it. If you feel nothing, rewrite it until you do. Emotion is the delivery mechanism.

Step One: Recall Your Top 10

What did last week’s exercises reveal about who you are?

Go back to your Achievements and Strengths Inventory from Week 2. Look at the words, qualities, and capabilities that came up most consistently. These are not aspirations. They are evidence. List your top ten descriptors below, alongside the achievement or context where each one showed up.

If a quality was named by others as well as yourself, it belongs here. These are the raw materials your affirmations will be built from.

Step Two: Affirmations from Your Descriptors

Each affirmation must trace back to a real strength in your top ten

Take three of your top ten descriptors, ideally ones you tend to discount or underuse, and build an affirmation directly from each one. The affirmation must be traceable: if someone asked you to prove that strength exists, you could point to the evidence in your inventory.

Step Three: Your Three Core Affirmations

Broader identity statements grounded in everything above

Using everything above as your foundation, write three broader affirmations that capture who you are at your best. These should feel like a statement of identity, not a goal. Rewrite until they produce a genuine response when you say them aloud.

Your Daily Practice

How to make these work

The research on affirmations is clear on one point: repetition is the mechanism. A single reading does nothing. Daily practice over weeks begins to shift the default narrative. The recommended minimum is once in the morning, spoken aloud, with attention on the felt response in the body rather than the words themselves.

Morning

Read your affirmations aloud. Attend to how each one lands in the body. Set the tone for the day with intention.

Midday

Pause for two minutes. Visualise yourself operating from the strength your affirmation describes. Make it specific and situational.

Evening

Identify one moment from the day where the strength showed up, however briefly. Acknowledge it before closing with your affirmations.

Reflection Questions
Which of your affirmations produces the strongest felt response when you say it aloud, and what does that tell you?
Which affirmation feels the least believable right now? What would need to be true for it to feel fully grounded?
What limiting belief is each affirmation directly countering? Name the belief underneath.
Where in your professional life would these affirmations make the most practical difference if they became your operating default?

Before your next session

Affirmations open a door. They do not walk through it for you. The subconscious patterns that generated the limiting beliefs in the first place are still running. Consistent practice weakens those patterns over time, but the deeper work of reprocessing them happens in session.

Bring your three core affirmations and your answers to the reflection questions to your next session. Notice which affirmations produced resistance and bring that resistance too. That is the most useful material we have.
