Developing Positive Behaviours

Grow in happiness and success through intentional action

Introduction

Your behaviour shapes how you feel.

Every action you take falls into one of four classes. Some feel good and do good. Some feel hard but build something worthwhile. Some feel good in the moment but quietly erode your wellbeing. And some feel bad and produce nothing of value at all.

Once you can see which class your behaviours belong to, you have the power to choose differently. This exercise helps you identify what you are already doing well, and helps you move Class 3 and 4 behaviours toward Class 1 and 2.

You are not stuck with the behaviours you have. You are stuck with the behaviours you keep choosing.

The Gold Standard
Feels good + Good outcome
Tap to explore

These behaviours create immediate satisfaction whilst benefiting everyone involved and building towards a better future.

Example: When a colleague makes a mistake, you reassure them, identify areas for improvement, and offer your support. This creates trust and psychological safety for everyone.

2
Investment in Excellence
Feels hard at first + Good outcome
Tap to explore

These behaviours require initial discomfort but yield significant long-term rewards. They are investments in your future self and a positive influence on those around you.

Example: Exercise feels difficult at first but builds health, energy, and resilience over time. Your discipline also inspires others to make better choices.

3
The Deceptive Trap
Feels good now + Bad outcome
Tap to explore

These behaviours offer immediate gratification but create long-term problems. The initial pleasure masks the underlying harm. They often become habitual because they provide instant relief.

Example: When stressed, reaching for alcohol or junk food gives brief relief but damages your health, sets a poor example, and avoids the real issue.

4
Worst of All Worlds
Feels bad + Bad outcome
Tap to explore

These behaviours offer no benefit whatsoever. They feel bad and create negative outcomes across every dimension. They often persist due to fear, avoidance, or habit.

Example: Staying silent when someone repeatedly hurts you feels easier in the moment but prevents any resolution, reinforces resentment, and leaves the situation unchanged.

Step 3 of 3
Move Toward Class 1 and 2

The need behind each behaviour is real and valid. The behaviour is just the wrong tool to meet it. For each of your Class 3 and 4 behaviours, find a Class 1 or 2 alternative that meets the same need in a way that actually serves you.

You do not need to eliminate a behaviour by willpower alone. Replace it with a specific, achievable Class 1 or 2 behaviour that meets the same underlying need. That is where lasting change begins.
